What is Crunch?

Crunch itself, is the action of pushing yourself, in computer science, and especially game development, this is usually defined by working very long hours. This is often caused by the need to meet a strict deadline, like a development team's inability to achieve specific milestones in time for a schedule. Programmers are pressured to work very long hours of overtime, which can be unpaid,[1] since payments may require programmers to meet said milestones.[2] What makes Crunch in the field stand out howevere, is that it can last for excrutiatingly long periods of time. Developers can always just avoid working overtime, it isn't necessarily always legally obligated for them to crunch, but it is nearly always an expectation. This expectation comes from an external source, mostly referred to as Crunch Culture.


The difference between Crunch and Crunch Culture

Crunch Culture is often used to describe settings in the video games industry, as it is where Crunch is at its most prominent within the field of computer science. This may seem like it does not affect a lot of people, but there are over 47,000 people employed in the games industry, within the United Kingdom alone.[3] Within the confines of Crunch Culture, developers are forced, directly or indirectly, to work overtime for what can amount to 80-100 hour weeks. If the developer is a contractor, that overtime will remain unpaid, as their salary is not affected by the number of hours they spend on the job. Failing to spend those hours leads to unlikely chances of having a contract renewed and being hired again. What specifically differentiates this from Crunch time, is that Crunch Culture normalizes this practice, and carries it out for long periods of time, from several months, to several years even. This is where the problem comes from, Crunch Culture suggests that regularly staying up late, working tirelessly on a project, is a good thing, and is greatly encouraged, even expected from those in the field. In the rare cases where Crunch isn't forced in one way or another, it still means that work due for a certain deadline will have to be completed, and if a certain developer decides to take time off, that means that extra work will fall into other people's hands,[4] which obviously reinforces the idea that Crunch is required to work in the industry.
